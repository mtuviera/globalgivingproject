Does a "Better" Environment Lead to More Giving?

A Data-to-Information Project Analyzing the Global Philanthropy Environment Index (GPEI) vs. the World Giving Index (WGI).

By Seamus DiGiorno & Maya Tuviera

Target User

Policymakers and NGO leaders who need to understand if improving government regulations actually results in increased public generosity, or if culture plays a bigger role.

Data Sources

Primary: Indiana University Global Philanthropy Environment Index (GPEI).
Secondary: CAF World Giving Index (WGI).

Analysis

Data to Information Journey

1. Global Generosity vs. Policy

Comparing geographic spread of policy scores (color) vs. giving behavior (size).

Key Insights:
  • The European Problem: Western Europe is light green (High Policy Score) but has relatively small bubbles, indicating low individual giving.
  • The Global South Leader: Southeast Asia (Indonesia) displays the largest bubbles despite lighter coloring.
  • US Alignment: The US shows a rare alignment where policy support matches public behavior.

2. Correlation Analysis

Is there a linear relationship between GPEI (X-axis) and WGI (Y-axis)?

Key Insights:
  • Weak Correlation: The data distribution is scattered, indicating that a "better" environment does not reliably predict higher giving.
  • The "Culture" Cluster: Top-left cluster (African/SE Asian nations) proves giving persists in restrictive environments.
  • The "Policy" Cluster: Bottom-right cluster (Europe) suggests high taxes may reduce the perceived need for charity.

3. Giving by Environment Category

Distribution of WGI scores across Low, Medium, and High GPEI environments.

Key Insights:
  • High Variance in 'Low' Env: Contains both the world's most generous and least generous countries.
  • Consistency in 'High' Env: Good policy creates a consistent "floor" for giving, but doesn't raise the "ceiling."
  • Median Similarity: "Medium" environments often outperform "High" environments.

4. Multivariate Factor Analysis

Comparing specific GPEI factors (Tax, Cross-Border) against Generosity.

Key Insights:
  • Tax Incentives: Positive correlation with "Donating Money," but zero correlation with "Helping a Stranger."
  • Cross-Border Friction: Countries blocking foreign funds (e.g., Nigeria) still maintain high domestic giving.
  • Regulatory Quality: "Ease of Operating" matters to NGOs but not to the average citizen donor.

5. Regional Generosity Gap

Average WGI (Blue) vs. GPEI (Red) scores by Region.

Key Insights:
  • The Africa Gap: Generosity (WGI) massively exceeds the Policy Environment (GPEI).
  • The European Gap: Policy Environment score is significantly higher than the actual Generosity score.
  • Southeast Asia Anomaly: Exceptional performance on both metrics relative to economic development.

6. Global Density Clusters

Where do the majority of the world's countries sit?

Key Insights:
  • The "Moderate" Trap: Most countries have decent policies but only moderate giving.
  • The Empty Quadrant: Very few countries exist in "Low Policy / Low Giving."
  • The Elite Corner: Only a few nations (USA, Aus, NZ, Singapore) reach the "High Policy / High Giving" sweet spot.

Methodology & Reflection

We merged two distinct datasets using ISO Country Codes. The GPEI data (Scale 1-5) was normalized to match the WGI percentage scale (0-100) to allow for direct comparison in the charts above. We filtered for countries present in both datasets (n=98).

The biggest challenge was handling the missing values for smaller nations that appeared in the World Giving Index but lacked a comprehensive Philanthropy Environment score. We had to decide whether to impute the average regional score or exclude them to maintain accuracy. We chose exclusion.

Discovering where policy behavior produced meaningful impacts on individual giving and where it did not was the most rewarding insight. It shows how governments can behave with regard to taxation and legislative policies in order to encourage individual philanthropic behavior on a national scale.

We retrieved our data from two sources, the Global Philanthropy Environment Index (GPEI) from Indiana University and the World Giving Index (WGI) from the Charities Aid Foundation (Kaggle Dataset).
